- **Age:** 37
- **Prior training level:** Advanced — 330 lb bench press prior to injury

## Injury summary

### Cervical radiculopathy

The client experienced a cervical radiculopathy at Thanksgiving 2025, most likely involving the C7 nerve root. The radicular symptoms (pain, possible sensory changes) resolved within approximately 8 weeks. However, a motor deficit persists in the left pectoralis major, specifically the sternal (lower) head. This is the primary ongoing concern.

### Facet joint flare (February 2026)

Approximately 10 weeks after the original radiculopathy onset, in mid-February 2026, the client developed an acute facet joint irritation in the cervical spine without clear provocation. This was a secondary flare related to residual segmental instability from the original injury, not a new injury or recurrence of the radiculopathy. It followed a normal resolution curve and fully resolved within ~2 weeks. No radicular symptoms returned during or after the flare.

The temporary restrictions documented under [Training guidelines](#training-guidelines) below applied during the acute flare. They are retained here for reference in the event of future flares.

## Left pectoral deficit — key details

### What is affected

- Left pectoralis major, sternal (lower) head — partially denervated and visibly atrophied
- Left pectoralis minor — largely intact
- Left pectoralis major, clavicular (upper) head — engages nearly fully; appears functional
- No obvious weakness detected in triceps, deltoid, biceps, grip, or other upper extremity muscles

### Suspected mechanism

The C7 nerve root injury affected the lateral pectoral nerve's supply to the pec major sternal head. The deficit appears to be a combination of two overlapping issues:

- **True denervation (estimated 30–40%):** Some nerve fibers were damaged, leaving a portion of muscle fibers without neural input. These fibers cannot contract voluntarily and require axonal regrowth or collateral sprouting to recover. This is a slow process (months).
- **Cortical inhibition (estimated 60–70%):** The brain downregulated motor drive to the muscle during the acute injury and has not fully restored it. These motor units are structurally intact but poorly recruited. This component responds to deliberate activation practice and can improve in weeks to months.

### Evidence for inhibition-dominant deficit

- Client has seen noticeable improvement in pec recruitment within days of focused activation work
- Improvement carried over between sessions (neural consolidation)
- Supporting the muscle manually during flexing produces a fuller contraction (proprioceptive facilitation)
- Compound movements (e.g., dumbbell bench press) feel relatively functional due to compensation, but isolated testing reveals significant weakness

### Current presentation

- Visible asymmetry: left lower pec appears flatter and less full than right
- Partial voluntary contraction present but incomplete
- Isolated pressing (single-arm wall press) reveals significant weakness
- Upper pec and triceps show compensatory overload/soreness from picking up slack
- Deep contraction near ribs present (pec minor), superficial contraction (pec major sternal head) diminished

## Medical workup in progress

The client is seeing a pain management specialist for follow-up. The following has been discussed and is being pursued:

- **EMG / Nerve conduction study:** Scheduled. Results expected ~3–4 weeks from intake date. To assess the degree of denervation, whether reinnervation is occurring, and to guide decisions about further intervention. Timing at 5+ months post-onset is appropriate for an informative study.
- **Possible updated cervical imaging:** To evaluate whether ongoing nerve root compression is present and whether surgical decompression may be indicated.
- **Electrical muscle stimulation (EMS):** Being considered as an adjunct to maintain muscle fiber viability while awaiting reinnervation.

**Important:** Training recommendations below should be adjusted based on EMG results when available. If significant ongoing denervation is found, the approach may need to be modified per medical guidance.

## Training guidelines

### Facet joint flare — temporary restrictions

These restrictions applied during the February 2026 acute flare and are retained for reference in the event of future flares. They do not currently apply.

- Avoid sustained cervical extension and combined extension/rotation
- Favor flexion-biased positioning during rest and exercise setup
- Gentle, rhythmic movement is beneficial (e.g., slow cat-cow in comfortable range, posterior pelvic tilts)
- Heavy axial loading should be limited until pain resolves
- Heat (moist) for 15–20 minutes before training can help reduce paraspinal guarding
- Restrictions can be progressively lifted as symptoms allow; full resolution typically within 2–3 weeks

### Left pec — rehabilitation training protocol

**Training philosophy:** The left sternal pec requires a fundamentally different approach than normal hypertrophy training. The goal is neural re-education and muscle fiber maintenance, not progressive overload. Treat it as rehab, not strength training, for now.

#### Core principles

- **Light load, high frequency:** 2–3 sets of isolation work, 4–5 times per week. Partially denervated muscle fatigues quickly. Frequent low-dose stimulation is superior to high-volume single sessions.
- **Mind-muscle connection is paramount:** If the client cannot feel the lower pec engaging, the weight is too heavy and compensators are taking over. Reduce load until contraction is perceptible.
- **Slow eccentrics (3–5 seconds):** Eccentric contractions recruit motor units differently and provide stronger stimulus for maintaining fiber integrity with reduced innervation.
- **Pre-contraction cueing:** Before each rep, have the client place his right hand on the lower left pec and attempt to contract it before initiating the movement. Tactile feedback significantly aids recruitment.
- **Isometric holds (10–15 seconds):** At peak contraction, hold and focus on contraction quality. Isometrics help re-establish the neural connection without the complexity of moving through range.
- **Frequent flexing throughout the day:** The client should voluntarily contract the left pec as often as possible outside of training (10–20 times per day). This has zero fatigue cost and reinforces cortical drive to the muscle. This is one of the most impactful interventions available.

#### Recommended isolation exercises (priority order)

1. **High-to-low cable crossover (left arm).** Cable anchored high on the left side. Left hand pulls down and across body toward right hip. No pressing component minimizes triceps involvement. Pause at bottom for 2–3 second squeeze. Use light resistance. Cue: drive with the elbow, not the hand.
2. **Supine single-arm squeeze press (light dumbbell).** Lying on back, single light dumbbell on left side. Focus on squeezing inward toward sternum throughout the press. Can also be done palm-to-palm against opposite hand for resistance in early stages.
3. **Pec deck / machine fly.** Machine removes stabilization demand. Very light weight, slow tempo, pause at peak contraction. Prevents deltoid from compensating.
4. **Floor fly with light dumbbell.** Floor limits ROM at bottom (where deltoid tends to take over). Focus on the squeezing/contracting portion.
5. **Single-arm wall press.** Useful for isolation and self-assessment. Vary hand height to shift emphasis between upper and lower fibers. Lower hand position biases the sternal head.

#### Upper pec (left clavicular head)

The left upper pec appears largely intact and is already responding to training stimulus (reports significant soreness after isolation work). This can be trained with normal progressive loading. It is currently compensating heavily for the lower pec deficit.

#### Compound pressing

Compound pressing (bench press, push-ups) can remain in the program for overall maintenance, but should not be relied upon as the primary stimulus for rebuilding the left lower pec. The client's nervous system effectively routes around the deficit during compound movements, masking the true extent of weakness. Dedicated isolation work is essential.

Heavy pressing (approaching previous working weights) should be deferred for approximately 6–9 months from original onset, pending nerve recovery progress and medical clearance.

### Spinal stabilization — preventing future flares

The February 2026 facet flare occurred because the deep spinal stabilizers (particularly multifidus) had not recovered from the original radiculopathy. Research shows multifidus atrophies rapidly after spinal pain and does not automatically recover when pain resolves. The superficial muscles compensate, but they are not designed for segmental stabilization, which leads to recurrent irritation.

A structured stabilization program should be initiated:

- Multifidus retraining: begin with isolated activation in prone or quadruped, progressing to sustained holds, then integration with limb movements (bird-dog progressions)
- Transversus abdominis co-activation with multifidus
- Gradual progression to functional positions (sitting, standing) and eventually loaded/dynamic activities
- A PT with spinal motor control experience is ideal for the initial learning phase, as tactile and/or ultrasound feedback significantly accelerates multifidus re-education

## Red flags — stop and refer back to physician

- Return of radiating pain into the arm in the same pattern as the original radiculopathy
- New or worsening numbness, tingling, or weakness in the arm or hand
- Progressive worsening of pec weakness or recruitment (getting worse, not better)
- Facet pain that is not improving or is worsening after 3 weeks
- Any bowel or bladder changes

Any of these warrant prompt medical re-evaluation.

## Tracking progress

- **Photos:** Every 2 weeks, same position, same lighting, both sides flexed. Compare over time for volume changes.
- **Contraction quality:** More important than size in the early phase. Note whether the client can feel the lower pec engaging more fully over time. This indicates reinnervation or cortical reconnection.
- **Compensator fatigue:** Track whether the left upper pec and triceps are getting less disproportionately sore from the same exercises. Decreasing compensator soreness = the lower pec is carrying more of its share.
- **Wall press test:** Periodic single-arm wall press as a standardized self-test. Note subjective strength and stability. This strips away compensations and shows true capacity.

## Expected prognosis

- **Months 3–5:** Most noticeable gains in recruitment quality. Brain reconnects with available motor units. Contraction feels more solid and responsive. Size changes may not be dramatic yet.
- **Months 4–8:** If reinnervation is occurring, visible volume returns. Droopiness gradually fills in. Strength improves tangibly during isolation work.
- **Months 8–14:** Approaching recovery ceiling. Muscle should look and function substantially better. May not reach perfect symmetry but functional strength should be well restored.

**Overall estimate:** 85–95% recovery is realistic given the positive early response to activation work, partial contraction present, and inhibition-dominant presentation. Return to heavy bench pressing (~330 lb range) is likely achievable within 9–14 months from original onset. Some subtle lower pec asymmetry may persist but should be cosmetically minor.
